International Relations begins with understanding its core components. These concepts are often used interchangeably, but they have distinct meanings.

1.4 The Players on the World Stage

While IR traditionally focused on states, the modern world is filled with a diverse range of influential actors.

üö©

State Actors

The primary actors in traditional IR. There are 195 states, each with territory, borders, and a claim to sovereignty‚Äîthe exclusive right to govern their territory.

üåê

Non-State Actors

Entities that are not states but play a significant role in global politics. Their rise marks a shift from 'inter-national' (between states) to 'trans-national' (across borders) affairs.

  • IGOs: International Governmental Organizations (e.g., The United Nations)
  • NGOs: Non-Governmental Organizations (e.g., Amnesty International)
  • MNCs: Multinational Corporations

1.5 & 1.6 The Rules of the Game: Levels & Structures

To analyze global events, we can use different "levels of analysis." These events unfold within a global system defined by three key concepts.

üîç

Levels of Analysis

A framework (popularized by Kenneth Waltz) to study IR from different perspectives:

  1. The Individual: Focus on leaders, their psychology and decisions.
  2. The State: Focus on a country's foreign policy and government type.
  3. The System: Focus on the "big picture"‚Äîthe distribution of power and international rules.
üëë

Sovereignty

A state's ultimate authority within its territory (internal) and its independence in the international community (external). It's the foundational principle of the state system.

‚ùì

Anarchy

The defining feature of the international system. It doesn't mean chaos, but the absence of a central, overarching authority or world government. States must rely on self-help.

‚öñÔ∏è

Power

"The currency of international politics." It is the ability of actor 'A' to get actor 'B' to do something it would not otherwise do. The distribution of power determines the system's polarity:

  • Unipolar: One dominant state.
  • Bipolar: Two dominant states (e.g., Cold War).
  • Multipolar: Several equal states.

1.7 Lenses for Understanding: Theories of IR

Theories are not facts; they are lenses that help us make sense of the complex world of international relations by focusing on different aspects of reality.

üïäÔ∏è Idealism / Liberalism

"Peace and harmony between nations is achievable and desirable."

  • Core Beliefs: Optimistic view of human nature. Democracies don't fight each other. Peace is advanced through international law, institutions (like the UN), and free trade.
  • Key Thinkers: Immanuel Kant, Woodrow Wilson.

ü¶Å Realism

"International politics... is a struggle for power." - Hans Morgenthau

  • Core Beliefs: Pessimistic view. The international system is anarchic. States are the main actors and are driven by self-interest and survival. Conflict is inevitable.
  • Key Thinkers: Thucydides, Machiavelli, Hobbes, Hans Morgenthau, Kenneth Waltz.

üîó Structuralism / Marxism

"The world is defined by economic inequality and exploitation."

  • Core Beliefs: The international system is a capitalist structure of inequality. It creates a division between the wealthy "core" (North) and the exploited "periphery" (South).
  • Key Thinkers: Karl Marx, Immanuel Wallerstein.

üí° Constructivism

"Anarchy is what states make of it." - Alexander Wendt

  • Core Beliefs: A middle-ground theory. Interests and identities are not fixed; they are "socially constructed" through interaction, ideas, values, and norms.
  • Key Thinkers: Alexander Wendt.
